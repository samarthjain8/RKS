Understanding Private Equity

What Is Private Equity?

Private Equity (PE) refers to equity capital invested directly into private companies — businesses not listed on any public stock exchange. PE investors provide growth capital, buyout financing, or turnaround funding in exchange for an ownership stake and, typically, board representation and governance influence.

Unlike public market investing, PE is a long-duration, hands-on asset class. Investors commit capital for 4–7 years, actively working with portfolio company management to drive operational improvement, strategic expansion, and ultimately a profitable exit — through an IPO, strategic sale, or secondary transaction.

"The best PE partnerships are built on shared ambition — an investor who believes in your business as much as you do, and has the capital and network to prove it."

For businesses, attracting the right PE partner is transformative — not just for the capital, but for the strategic expertise, industry networks, and operational rigour they bring. RKS Investments helps companies identify, attract, and negotiate with the investors best aligned to their growth vision.

Who Invests

Types of Private Capital Investors

Not all private capital is the same. Understanding the investor universe — their mandates, ticket sizes, return expectations, and involvement styles — is essential to running a successful fundraise. RKS Investments navigates this landscape on your behalf.

PE Funds

Private Equity Funds

Institutional funds that manage pools of capital from LPs (pension funds, sovereign funds, HNIs) and invest for returns over a 4–7 year horizon. Active board involvement and governance improvement are hallmarks of PE funds.

  • Ticket size: ₹25 Cr – ₹500 Cr+
  • Stage: Growth, buyout, turnaround
  • Horizon: 4–7 years
  • Involvement: High — board seat typical
  • Return target: 20–30% IRR
Venture Capital

Venture Capital Funds

VC funds invest in early-to-growth stage startups with high-growth potential, accepting higher risk for potentially exponential returns. VC investors bring ecosystem access, mentorship, and follow-on capital capabilities.

  • Ticket size: ₹2 Cr – ₹100 Cr
  • Stage: Seed, Series A/B/C
  • Horizon: 5–10 years
  • Involvement: Medium — mentorship & advisory
  • Return target: 3–10x on portfolio
Family Offices

Family Offices & HNIs

Ultra-high-net-worth families and their dedicated investment offices who invest directly into private companies — often with longer time horizons, more flexible terms, and a preference for sectors aligned with their operating expertise.

  • Ticket size: ₹5 Cr – ₹150 Cr
  • Stage: Growth, pre-IPO, buyout
  • Horizon: Flexible — 3–10 years
  • Involvement: Low-to-medium
  • Return target: 15–25% IRR
Strategic Investors

Strategic Corporate Investors

Large corporates or conglomerates that invest for strategic benefits — market access, technology acquisition, or competitive positioning — in addition to financial returns. They often become eventual acquirers.

  • Ticket size: Flexible
  • Stage: Growth, buyout, JV
  • Horizon: Strategic, not fixed
  • Involvement: High — often leads to M&A
  • Return target: Strategic + financial
AIFs

Alternative Investment Funds

SEBI-registered Category I & II AIFs pool capital from accredited investors to deploy into unlisted companies, real assets, and structured credit. Growing rapidly as India's primary vehicle for domestic PE investing.

  • Ticket size: ₹10 Cr – ₹300 Cr
  • Stage: Growth, infrastructure, distressed
  • Horizon: 5–7 years (fund life)
  • Involvement: Medium — board observer typical
  • Return target: 18–28% IRR
Sovereign / DFI

Sovereign Funds & DFIs

Government-backed sovereign wealth funds (GIC, ADIA, Temasek) and Development Finance Institutions (IFC, ADB, DEG) deploy large-scale patient capital into growth markets like India, often with ESG and impact mandates.

  • Ticket size: ₹200 Cr – ₹5,000 Cr+
  • Stage: Late-stage growth, infrastructure
  • Horizon: 7–12 years
  • Involvement: Low — minority, passive
  • Return target: 12–20% IRR
Investment Stage

Stages of Private Capital Investment

A

Angel / Pre-Seed

₹25L – ₹3 Cr

Earliest stage — typically friends, family, and angel investors funding the initial product build and market validation. High risk, high reward, minimal governance.

S

Seed Stage

₹1 Cr – ₹15 Cr

Post-concept, pre-revenue or early-revenue stage. Seed funds and micro-VCs fund team building, product development, and initial go-to-market execution.

A

Series A

₹10 Cr – ₹100 Cr

First institutional round. Company has demonstrated product-market fit and early revenue. Capital deployed toward scaling the team, product, and customer acquisition.

G

Growth Equity

₹50 Cr – ₹500 Cr

Established, profitable or near-profitable businesses raising capital to scale operations, expand geographically, or fund acquisitions without relinquishing control.

P

Pre-IPO Round

₹50 Cr – ₹500 Cr

Capital raised 12–24 months before a planned IPO — from institutional investors seeking to enter at a discount to the anticipated listing price with full liquidity post-listing.

PE Exit Strategies

01

Initial Public Offering

Listing the company on BSE or NSE allows PE investors to exit through the public markets — typically the highest-value exit route due to public market liquidity premium and broad investor base.

BSE / NSE listing OFS in IPO Lock-in post-listing
02

Strategic Sale

Selling the company or a majority stake to a strategic acquirer — a larger corporate, global player, or industry consolidator — who values the business for strategic fit beyond pure financial returns.

Full or partial exit Control premium M&A process
03

Secondary Transaction

The PE investor sells its stake to another financial investor — a larger PE fund, a secondary fund, or a new strategic investor — without involving the operating company in the transaction.

GP-led secondaries LP stake sale Continuation funds
04

Promoter Buyback

The founding promoters repurchase the PE investor's stake — typically after the company has generated sufficient cash flows or obtained debt financing to fund the buyout of the investor.

Management buyout Put option exercise Debt-funded

Key PE Deal Terms Explained

CCPS

Compulsorily Convertible Preference Shares

The most common PE investment instrument in India. CCPS gives investors preference in dividends and liquidation while converting to equity at a pre-agreed ratio — balancing downside protection with upside participation.

Anti-D

Anti-Dilution Protection

A provision protecting investors from dilution in a down-round. Broad-based weighted average anti-dilution is founder-friendly; full-ratchet anti-dilution is investor-friendly and should be resisted.

LiqPref

Liquidation Preference

Determines the order and amount investors receive upon a liquidation or sale event. 1x non-participating is standard; participating preferred and multiple liquidation preferences are red flags for founders.

ROFO

Right of First Offer

Requires a shareholder to offer their shares to existing investors before selling to a third party. The investor has the right to make the first bid — preventing unexpected third-party entry.

Drag

Drag-Along Rights

Allows a majority shareholder (often the PE investor) to force minority shareholders to participate in a sale on the same terms — ensuring a clean exit when a majority decides to sell.

Tag

Tag-Along Rights

Protects minority shareholders (often the promoters or early investors) by allowing them to sell their shares at the same price and terms when a majority shareholder triggers a sale.

IRR

Internal Rate of Return

The annualised rate of return a PE investor expects to earn on their investment — typically 20–30% for Indian PE. IRR is the primary performance metric against which fund managers are evaluated.

ESOP

Employee Stock Option Pool

A portion of equity reserved for employee compensation. PE investors typically require a 10–15% ESOP pool to be created (diluting founders) before investment — incentivising key talent retention.

WC

Weighted Average Cost of Capital

The blended cost of a company's capital (debt + equity), used as the discount rate in DCF valuations. A lower WACC implies a higher company value — making capital structure optimisation important pre-raise.
