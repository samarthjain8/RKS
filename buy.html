Share Buyback

What Is a Share Buyback?

A share buyback — also called a share repurchase — is a transaction in which a listed company purchases its own shares from the open market or from existing shareholders, and subsequently extinguishes those shares, reducing the total outstanding share count. It is one of the most powerful capital allocation tools available to a well-capitalised listed company.

By reducing the number of shares in circulation, a buyback increases earnings per share (EPS), return on equity (ROE), and often the share price — directly benefiting remaining shareholders who do not tender their shares. It also signals management's conviction that the company's shares are undervalued, which in itself is a powerful market communication.

"A buyback is management's clearest public statement that they believe the stock is worth more than the market currently offers — and they're willing to bet capital on it."

In India, buybacks are governed by SEBI (Buy-Back of Securities) Regulations, 2018, and Companies Act Section 68–70 — with specific rules on buyback limits, pricing, method, timelines, and post-buyback restrictions on further capital market activities.

Why Companies Do Buybacks

01
Return Surplus Cash to Shareholders
More tax-efficient than dividends in many structures; shareholders can choose to participate or retain shares.
02
Signal Undervaluation to the Market
Management publicly communicating confidence in intrinsic value above current market price — a powerful positive signal.
03
Improve Key Per-Share Metrics
EPS, BPS, ROE, and ROCE all improve mechanically when the share count falls — making financial results look better to analysts.
04
Increase Promoter Holding %
If promoters do not tender, their percentage holding automatically increases as the total share count falls — without additional investment.
05
ESOP Share Management
Buying back shares to offset dilution from ESOP exercises — maintaining the share count and preventing option-exercise dilution for existing shareholders.
Method Selection

Buyback Methods Under SEBI Regulations

Most Common

Tender Offer (Fixed Price)

The company announces a fixed buyback price (at a premium to CMP) and invites shareholders to tender their shares within a specified window. Oversubscription is managed by proportionate acceptance. This is the most commonly used method for large buybacks by listed companies.

  • Buyback price fixed upfront — typically 20–30% premium to CMP
  • 15-working-day acceptance window
  • Proportionate acceptance if oversubscribed
  • Promoters and PAC can participate
  • Max buyback size: 25% of paid-up capital + free reserves
Flexible

Open Market (Stock Exchange)

The company buys its own shares from the open secondary market through the stock exchange order book — over a period of up to 6 months — at market-prevailing prices within a specified maximum price limit. No fixed price announced; purchases made daily at management discretion.

  • Purchases over up to 6 months on exchange
  • Maximum price ceiling pre-disclosed
  • Promoters and insiders cannot sell during window
  • 10% daily volume cap on exchange purchases
  • Suitable for smaller, flexible buybacks
Specialised

Odd Lot Buyback

A targeted buyback exclusively for shareholders holding less than the market lot (typically less than 100 shares) — giving small shareholders an exit at a fair price and reducing the company's shareholder count. Often used as a secondary objective alongside a larger tender offer.

  • Only shareholders holding odd lots eligible
  • Provides liquidity for otherwise illiquid small holdings
  • Reduces shareholder servicing costs for company
  • Often combined with a general tender offer
  • Separate acceptance ratio computed for odd-lot holders
Regulatory Conditions

Buyback Limits &
SEBI Conditions

SEBI and the Companies Act impose strict limits and conditions on buybacks — designed to prevent market manipulation, protect minority shareholders, and ensure that companies do not buy back shares using borrowed funds or beyond their financial capacity.

A critical condition is the debt-equity ratio limit: post-buyback, the company's total secured and unsecured debt cannot exceed twice its paid-up capital plus free reserves. This ensures buybacks are funded only from genuine surplus capital — not by taking on additional leverage.

RKS Investments conducts a comprehensive pre-buyback eligibility assessment — confirming that the company satisfies all SEBI and Companies Act conditions before any board or shareholder approval is sought.

⚠ Post-Buyback Restriction

A company cannot issue new shares or raise fresh capital (QIP, rights issue, preferential allotment) for a period of one year following the completion of a buyback — other than through an ESOP. This must be factored into capital planning before the buyback is announced.

Buyback Limit
Max 25% of paid-up equity capital + free reserves in a financial year Board-approved buybacks are capped at 10% of paid-up capital. Beyond 10% requires shareholder special resolution.
Debt-Equity Ratio
Post-buyback D/E ratio must not exceed 2:1 Debt = all secured + unsecured borrowings. Equity = paid-up capital + free reserves. Ensures buyback is from genuine surplus.
Source of Funds
Only from free reserves, securities premium, or proceeds of any earlier issue Buyback cannot be funded by fresh borrowings — only from accumulated internal accruals.
Minimum Buyback
At least 50% of the announced buyback amount must be completed If the company fails to complete 50% of the announced buyback, SEBI imposes restrictions on future buybacks.
Escrow Requirement
25% of the total buyback amount must be deposited in escrow before announcement Ensures the company has genuine funds committed before making a public buyback offer.
Post-Buyback Lock-in
No fresh equity issuance for 1 year post-buyback Applies to QIP, preferential allotment, rights issue — ESOP grants are exempt from this restriction.
Share Extinguishment
Bought-back shares must be extinguished within 7 days of closure Shares cannot be held as treasury stock — they must be permanently cancelled, reducing the total share count.
Pricing & Taxation

Buyback Pricing & Tax Treatment

For tender offer buybacks, the company announces a fixed price — which must be at a premium to the prevailing CMP and comply with the SEBI floor price formula (higher of 26-week or 2-week VWAP on the stock exchange). The price is fixed throughout the acceptance window.

For open market buybacks, no single price is announced — the company's broker purchases shares at market prices over the buyback window, subject to the pre-announced maximum price ceiling. Daily disclosure of purchases is mandatory.

From a tax perspective, buyback consideration is taxed in the hands of the company — not the individual shareholder — under Section 115QA of the Income Tax Act. The company pays a flat 20% buyback tax (plus surcharge and cess) on the distributed income (consideration minus cost of acquisition), making the exit tax-efficient for shareholders who tender.

Note: Budget 2024 Amendment

From 1st October 2024, the taxation of buybacks has been restructured: buyback proceeds in the hands of shareholders are treated as deemed dividends, taxable at applicable slab rates. The company-level Section 115QA tax has been removed. Advisors and companies must model the post-Budget tax impact before structuring a buyback.

Tender Price
Fixed at announcement — typically 20–35% premium to CMP Must not be lower than the higher of: (a) highest price paid in last 12 months, (b) VWAP-based formula per SEBI.
Open Market Price
Market price at time of purchase, subject to max ceiling Max price ceiling disclosed upfront. No purchases within 10 trading days of quarterly results or price-sensitive announcements.
Shareholder Tax (Pre-Oct 2024)
Nil — company pays 20%+SC+Cess under Section 115QA Legacy buybacks: shareholders receive consideration tax-free; company bears the buyback tax.
Shareholder Tax (Post-Oct 2024)
Proceeds taxable as deemed dividend at applicable slab rates Significant impact on high-slab shareholders who tender. Full tax modelling required before any buyback announcement.
Cost Adjustment
Capital loss available on cost of tendered shares post-Oct 2024 Loss from cost of acquisition of tendered shares can be set off against capital gains in the same or future years.

Voluntary Delisting

Advisory for promoters seeking to take their listed company private through a voluntary delisting offer — one of the most complex and high-stakes transactions in Indian capital markets.

Voluntary Delisting

What Is Voluntary Delisting?

Voluntary delisting is the process by which a company's promoters seek to buy out the remaining public shareholders and have the company's shares removed from the stock exchange — effectively taking the company private. It is initiated by the promoter or major shareholder group who wish to own 100% of the company without the obligations of being publicly listed.

Unlike compulsory delisting (which is imposed by regulators as a penalty), voluntary delisting is a commercially driven decision — motivated by the desire to operate without the disclosure obligations, compliance costs, and short-term market pressures of a listed company, or as a precursor to a major restructuring or foreign acquisition.

"Delisting is not a retreat — it is a strategic choice to reclaim the freedom to operate without the constraints of public markets, when being private serves the business better than being listed."

The delisting process is governed by SEBI (Delisting of Equity Shares) Regulations, 2021 — which prescribe a detailed, shareholder-protective process including a reverse book building mechanism, minimum acceptance threshold, and independent director oversight.

Why Promoters Choose to Delist

◆
Eliminate Listing Compliance Costs
Annual audit, LODR filings, investor relations, board meetings, and governance compliance are expensive and time-consuming for companies with limited public float benefit.
◆
Remove Minority Shareholder Constraints
Strategic decisions — restructuring, asset sales, related-party transactions — require minority approval in listed entities. Going private removes these constraints.
◆
Strategic Restructuring or M&A
Foreign parent acquiring an Indian listed subsidiary often requires delisting as part of the acquisition structure — to gain full control and simplify group governance.
◆
Low Public Float — Illiquid Listing
Companies with less than 25% public float or thin trading volumes derive little benefit from being listed — high compliance cost with no meaningful capital market access.
◆
Undervalued by Public Markets
If a company's intrinsic value is significantly higher than its traded price, delisting allows the promoter to acquire the public float at a discount to true value — a value-accretive transaction.
RBB Mechanism

Reverse Book Building —
The Delisting Price Discovery Mechanism

The Reverse Book Building (RBB) mechanism is the heart of the Indian voluntary delisting process. Unlike a conventional IPO (where the company discovers a price at which to sell shares), in RBB the public shareholders collectively discover the minimum price at which they are willing to sell their shares to the promoter.

Each shareholder submits a bid — specifying how many shares they are tendering and the minimum price per share they are willing to accept. The system aggregates all bids and determines the "discovered price" — the price at which the maximum number of shares are tendered such that the promoter can cross the 90% threshold required to delist.

The promoter is not bound to accept the discovered price — they can accept it (and proceed to delist) or reject it (if the price is too high) and the delisting fails. This creates a negotiation dynamic between the promoter and public shareholders that requires careful strategy and pricing advisory.

⚠ The 90% Threshold Rule

For a delisting to succeed, the promoter must hold or acquire at least 90% of the total issued and paid-up equity capital of the company through the RBB process. If the total post-RBB promoter holding falls below 90%, the delisting fails — regardless of the price bid. Achieving 90% is both the pricing challenge and the execution challenge in every delisting.

How Reverse Book Building Works
1
Floor Price Set
SEBI formula determines the minimum price — higher of: 26W VWAP, 2W VWAP, highest price paid in last 26W, and book value. Promoter cannot accept below this floor.
2
Shareholders Bid
Each public shareholder bids at or above the floor price — specifying the number of shares and minimum acceptable price. Bidding window is open for 5 working days.
3
Discovered Price Determined
System aggregates bids by price. Discovered price = the price at which cumulative shares tendered first crosses the 90% threshold target.
4
Promoter Accepts or Rejects
Promoter has 2 working days to accept or reject the discovered price. If accepted at or above floor price and 90% threshold is met — delisting proceeds.
5
Exit Offer for Remaining Holders
Post-delisting, shares are traded for 6 months on the exchange under "Exit Offer" provisions — giving remaining public shareholders an exit at the delisting price.

Escrow & Public Announcement

The promoter deposits 100% of the maximum consideration payable (discovered price × total public shares) in an escrow account with a scheduled bank — demonstrating full financial commitment to the delisting. Reverse Book Building Window

The RBB window opens for 5 working days. Shareholders bid at their desired minimum exit price via their brokers through the exchange bidding platform. The discovered price is computed at close of bidding based on cumulative bids. Promoter Acceptance & Settlement

The promoter accepts or rejects the discovered price within 2 working days. If accepted and 90% threshold is met: settlement proceeds, consideration paid to tendering shareholders, shares transferred to promoter, and exchange proceeds to delist within 15 working days. If rejected: escrow released, RBB process fails, and shares continue to trade.

Post-delisting, the promoter must provide an exit opportunity to remaining public shareholders (those who did not participate in the RBB) — at the delisting price — for a period of 1 year after the delisting date. This ensures no shareholder is involuntarily trapped without an exit mechanism.

Regulatory Framework

Delisting Regulatory
Requirements

The voluntary delisting framework in India was significantly overhauled by SEBI's 2021 Delisting Regulations — which introduced the Fixed Price route as an alternative to the pure RBB mechanism, enhanced escrow requirements, and tightened the timeline and disclosure standards.

The 2021 regulations also introduced the concept of "delisting through scheme of arrangement" — allowing companies to bypass the full RBB process in certain merger and demerger contexts where a NCLT-approved scheme effectively results in the buyout of public shareholders.

RKS Investments monitors SEBI's evolving delisting framework in real time and advises promoters on the most compliant and cost-effective route available given their specific shareholding structure and strategic objectives.
