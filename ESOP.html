Understanding ESOPs

What Is an ESOP?

An Employee Stock Option Plan (ESOP) is a compensation mechanism that gives employees the right — but not the obligation — to purchase shares of their employer at a predetermined price (the exercise or grant price) after completing a specified vesting period. It is one of the most powerful tools available to companies for aligning employee interests with long-term shareholder value creation.

For employees, ESOPs offer the potential to share in the wealth they help create — participating in the company's equity appreciation beyond their salary. For employers, ESOPs provide a non-cash compensation lever that conserves capital while creating strong retention incentives and a culture of ownership.

"An ESOP done right turns your best people into co-owners — aligning effort, ambition, and reward in a way that no salary or bonus ever can."

However, ESOPs are complex instruments governed by multiple overlapping regulations — the Companies Act, SEBI (Share Based Employee Benefits) Regulations, Income Tax Act, and Ind AS 102. A poorly designed or improperly implemented ESOP can create legal exposure, accounting complications, and employee dissatisfaction rather than motivation. Expert advisory is essential from day one.

The ESOP Lifecycle

Grant
Option Grant

Company grants options to eligible employees at an exercise price (grant price). Board and compensation committee approval required.

Vesting
Vesting Period

Options vest over a schedule — typically 1–4 years with a cliff. Unvested options lapse on resignation.

Exercise
Option Exercise

Vested employee pays the exercise price and receives shares. Exercise window is typically 1–3 years post-vesting.

Taxation
Tax at Exercise

Perquisite tax arises at exercise on the spread (FMV minus exercise price). Employer deducts TDS at applicable slab rates.

Liquidity
Share Sale or Buyback

Employee sells shares on market (listed cos) or via secondary, buyback, or IPO (unlisted cos) to realise gains. Capital gains tax applies.

Reporting
Accounting & Disclosure

Annual ESOP expense recognised under Ind AS 102. Disclosures in annual report, directors' report, and financial statements.

Strategic Value

Why Companies Implement ESOPs

01
◆

Talent Retention

Vesting schedules create powerful "golden handcuffs" — employees with unvested options have a strong financial incentive to stay through their vesting period, reducing costly attrition at the worst possible times.

02
◆

Competitive Talent Attraction

ESOPs allow companies — particularly startups and growth-stage businesses — to attract senior talent who might otherwise demand higher cash compensation, by offering equity upside in lieu of immediate salary premium.

03
◆

Alignment of Interests

When employees become shareholders, they think and act like owners — making decisions that maximise long-term company value rather than short-term personal convenience. Culture and performance improve measurably.

04
◆

Cash-Efficient Compensation

ESOPs allow companies to compensate key employees with equity upside rather than cash outflow — preserving working capital during high-growth phases when cash is the most precious resource.

05
◆

Investor & PE Expectation

PE/VC investors and acquirers increasingly expect a well-structured ESOP pool as part of the management incentivisation framework before they invest or acquire — making ESOPs a prerequisite for institutional capital.

06
◆

Succession & Founder Transition

ESOPs help founders transition management to professional teams by creating long-term ownership stakes for successor leaders — aligning their financial interests with the continued success of the business post-transition.

Plan Structures

Types of Employee
Equity Compensation

Most Common

Employee Stock Options (ESOP)

The right to buy shares at a fixed exercise price after vesting. The employee profits from the difference between exercise price and market value at exercise — and bears no risk if the price falls below exercise price (options simply lapse).

  • Exercise price typically set at grant-date FMV
  • Vesting period: 1–4 years, with cliff
  • Taxed as perquisite at exercise
  • Suitable for all company stages
For Senior Leaders

Restricted Stock Units (RSUs)

A promise to deliver shares (or cash equivalent) after vesting conditions are met — without requiring the employee to pay an exercise price. RSUs have guaranteed value above zero, making them more attractive than options in mature or listed companies.

  • No exercise price — fully vested RSUs deliver shares
  • Taxed as perquisite on vesting (FMV at vest)
  • Stronger retention tool for CXO-level employees
  • Common in MNCs, listed companies
Founders / Advisors

Sweat Equity Shares

Shares issued at a discount or for non-cash consideration (intellectual property, know-how, value additions) to employees or directors as recognition of their contribution to the company — governed by Companies Act Section 54.

  • Issued at a discount to FMV — or for non-cash
  • Board resolution + special resolution required
  • 15% cap of paid-up capital (unless otherwise approved)
  • Lock-in of 3 years from allotment
Virtual / Unlisted

Phantom Stock & SARs

Synthetic equity instruments that pay out cash equal to the appreciation in share value — without issuing actual shares. Ideal for companies that cannot or do not wish to issue real equity, while still providing meaningful upside participation.

  • No dilution to existing shareholders
  • Payout tied to value appreciation
  • Taxed as salary income at payout
  • Suitable for subsidiaries, JVs, pre-corporate entities
Terminology

Key ESOP Terms Explained

Grant

Grant Date & Grant Price

The date on which options are awarded to an employee, and the price at which they may exercise those options. The grant price is typically set at Fair Market Value on the grant date — often determined by an independent valuation.

Vest

Vesting Schedule

The timeline over which options become exercisable. Most Indian ESOPs use a 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff — meaning 25% vests after Year 1, and the remainder vests monthly or quarterly over Years 2–4.

Cliff

Cliff Vesting

A minimum service period before any options vest. A 1-year cliff means no options vest in the first 12 months — protecting the company from option grants to employees who leave shortly after joining.

Pool

ESOP Pool Size

The percentage of fully diluted share capital reserved for ESOP grants. Indian companies typically reserve 5–15% for employees; PE/VC investors often require a pool to be created pre-investment, diluting founders.

FMV

Fair Market Value

The value of a share as determined by an independent valuation for the purpose of setting exercise price, computing perquisite tax at exercise, and recognising ESOP expense under Ind AS 102.

Lapse

Option Lapse

Unvested options lapse (are forfeited) on resignation, termination, or death. Vested but unexercised options typically have a 30–90 day post-separation exercise window before lapsing permanently.

Spread

Spread (Intrinsic Value)

The difference between the FMV of the share at exercise and the exercise price paid by the employee. This spread is taxed as a perquisite — a benefit in kind — under the Income Tax Act at the employee's slab rate.

Accel

Acceleration

A provision that allows unvested options to vest early — typically upon a change of control (acquisition), IPO, or death/disability. Single-trigger acceleration vests on one event; double-trigger requires two conditions.

Perq

Perquisite Taxation

At exercise, the spread (FMV at exercise minus exercise price) is taxed as a perquisite — treated as additional salary income — in the hands of the employee. The employer must deduct TDS at the applicable income tax slab rate.

Legal & Compliance

The Regulatory
Framework for ESOPs

ESOPs in India sit at the intersection of corporate law, securities regulation, tax law, and accounting standards — each imposing distinct requirements on scheme design, approval, disclosure, valuation, and accounting treatment.

For listed companies, SEBI's Share Based Employee Benefits and Sweat Equity Regulations (SBEB & SE), 2021 prescribe detailed requirements for scheme structure, disclosure, independent trustee obligations, and annual reporting. For unlisted private companies, the Companies Act and associated rules govern the scheme — with somewhat greater flexibility but no less rigour.

RKS Investments ensures every ESOP scheme is compliant across all applicable regulatory dimensions before implementation — and that ongoing obligations (annual valuations, disclosures, tax deductions) are met throughout the scheme's life.

Companies Act
Section 62(1)(b) — employee shares; Section 54 — sweat equity Board resolution + special resolution of shareholders required. Companies (Share Capital) Rules apply.
SEBI SBEB & SE
Listed companies — SEBI (SBEB & SE) Regulations, 2021 Requires independent trust, compliance officer, annual report disclosures, and shareholder approval by special resolution.
Income Tax Act
Section 17(2)(vi) — perquisite tax at exercise; Section 112A — LTCG on sale Spread taxed as perquisite at exercise; TDS obligation on employer; startup tax deferral available under Section 80-IAC.
Startup Tax Deferral
DPIIT-recognised startups — deferral of perquisite tax up to 5 years Tax payable within 14 days of: (a) sale of shares, (b) leaving company, or (c) 48 months from end of grant year — whichever is earliest.
Ind AS 102
Share-Based Payment — accounting for ESOP expense Fair value of options at grant date recognised as employee compensation expense over vesting period. Annual third-party valuation required.
FEMA
Cross-border ESOPs — RBI & FEMA compliance for foreign parent grants Indian employees receiving options in foreign parent companies require RBI permission and annual reporting under FEMA 19.

Design Choices

ESOP Scheme Design
Parameters

The structure of an ESOP scheme determines its effectiveness as a retention, incentive, and culture-building tool. Every parameter — from pool size to vesting schedule to exercise price — involves trade-offs between employee motivation, dilution management, tax efficiency, and regulatory compliance.

RKS Investments designs ESOP schemes around the specific objectives of each company — taking into account the founder's dilution tolerance, investor expectations, employee seniority mix, and stage of business. We model the full cap table impact of each design choice before any decision is finalised.

We also design schemes that can evolve — with provisions for top-up grants, performance vesting, acceleration on exit, and migration from unlisted to listed company status without requiring a complete scheme overhaul.

Pool Size
5% – 15% of fully diluted share capital PE investors typically require 10% minimum. Too small a pool limits grant flexibility; too large creates dilution.
Vesting Period
Typically 3–4 years with 1-year cliff Longer vesting = stronger retention. Shorter vesting = faster employee motivation. Performance vesting is increasingly common.
Exercise Price
FMV, par value, or discounted FMV At-FMV grants minimise perquisite tax at exercise. Par value grants create larger spread and higher tax but greater upside for employees.
Exercise Window
1–3 years post full vesting; 30–90 days post separation Longer exercise windows are employee-friendly; short windows post-separation protect the company from lingering outstanding options.
Eligibility
Permanent employees; can exclude part-time, contract, and board-only directors SEBI SBEB regulations specify eligible categories for listed companies; unlisted companies have flexibility.
Acceleration
Single-trigger (on change of control) or double-trigger (CoC + termination) Double-trigger acceleration is standard best practice — protects employees post-acquisition without incentivising premature exits.
Liquidity Mechanism
IPO, buyback, secondary sale, or drag-along for unlisted cos Critical for unlisted companies — without a defined liquidity path, ESOPs lose motivational value for employees.
Vesting Structures

Common Vesting Schedule Designs

Standard

4-Year Graded with 1-Year Cliff

The most widely used structure in India. No vesting for the first year (cliff), then 25% vests at month 12, followed by equal monthly or quarterly vesting over years 2–4.

Month 0–12: 0% vests (cliff period)
Month 12: 25% vests
Months 13–48: Remaining 75% vests monthly (2.08% per month)
Back-Weighted

4-Year Back-Loaded Vesting

A back-loaded schedule (e.g., 10/20/30/40 over four years) creates increasing retention incentives as the employee approaches the final year — when the largest tranche vests.

Year 1: 10% vests
Year 2: 20% vests
Year 3: 30% vests
Year 4: 40% vests
Performance

Performance-Based Vesting

Options vest based on achievement of specific performance targets — revenue milestones, EBITDA, or individual KPIs — in addition to or instead of time-based vesting. Aligns rewards with results.

Target: 100% vesting at 100% goal
Threshold: 50% vesting at 80% of goal
Stretch: 125–150% vesting at 120%+ of goal
Below threshold: 0% vesting for the year
Financial Impact

ESOP Accounting & Tax Treatment

Accounting under Ind AS 102

Company Books
Fair Value
Options valued at grant date using Black-Scholes or Binomial model Requires independent valuation — inputs include share price, exercise price, volatility, risk-free rate, expected life, dividend yield.
Expense Recognition
Grant-date fair value amortised as employee cost over vesting period Debit: Employee compensation expense | Credit: Share option reserve (equity). Non-cash — no cash outflow.
Modification
Repricing or scheme modification requires incremental fair value computation Any increase in fair value at modification date must be recognised as additional expense.
Forfeiture
Lapsed/forfeited options reverse previously recognised expense Cumulative expense recognised for unvested options is reversed in the period of lapse.
Exercise
On exercise — share capital and securities premium account credited Share option reserve transferred to securities premium. No P&L impact at exercise.

Tax Treatment — Employees

Employee Hands
At Grant
No tax — options are not taxable at grant date Tax event arises only at exercise — not when options are granted or vested.
At Vesting
No tax — vesting alone does not trigger a tax event Tax arises when the employee actually exercises (pays exercise price and receives shares).
At Exercise
Perquisite tax on spread (FMV at exercise minus exercise price) Taxed as salary income at applicable income tax slab. Employer must deduct TDS. Cost of acquisition = FMV at exercise.
At Sale
Capital gains on appreciation from FMV at exercise to sale price STCG (held <24 months unlisted; <12 months listed) or LTCG at applicable rates. Listed LTCG exempt up to ₹1.25L.
Startup Deferral
DPIIT-recognised startups — perquisite tax deferred up to 5 years from grant Tax payable on earliest of: 5 years from grant, share sale, or ceasing employment. Requires employer Form 12BAA filing.
Structural Mechanism

ESOP Trust — Structure
& Benefits

For listed companies and many PE-backed private companies, ESOPs are implemented through an employee welfare trust — a separate legal entity that holds the company's shares on behalf of employees. Under SEBI SBEB regulations, listed companies are required to route most equity compensation through an independent trust.

The trust structure provides several important advantages: it separates the ESOP administration from the company's direct share capital, allows secondary market purchases of shares (in addition to fresh issue), provides an independent trustee obligation that protects employee interests, and simplifies the mechanics of exercise and buyback.

RKS Investments advises on trust setup, trustee selection, trust deed drafting, SEBI compliance, and the ongoing obligations of the trust — including annual reporting, secondary market transactions, and interaction with the company's compensation committee.

◆
Trust Formation & Registration

Setting up the employee welfare trust as an irrevocable trust under the Indian Trusts Act, registering with the relevant sub-registrar, and obtaining a PAN for the trust entity.

◆
Independent Trustee Appointment

SEBI SBEB requires an independent trustee (not connected to the company) to administer the trust and act in employees' interests — particularly in listed company schemes.

◆
Trust Funding & Share Acquisition

The company provides a loan to the trust (or the trust raises financing) to purchase shares — either through fresh issue from the company or secondary market purchases of listed shares.

◆
Exercise & Delivery Mechanics

Upon exercise, the trust delivers shares to the employee's demat account — receiving the exercise price and facilitating TDS deduction coordination with payroll.

◆
Annual Trust Compliance

Annual accounts, audit, trustee meeting minutes, ROC filings, SEBI disclosures (for listed companies), and income tax filing for the trust entity — all coordinated by our team.

ESOP vs. Other Equity
Compensation Instruments

Instrument Employee Dilution Exercise Price Tax at Exercise / Vest Risk to Employee Best Suited For
Stock Options (ESOP) Dilutive — new shares Fixed at grant date (typically FMV) Perquisite tax on spread at exercise Options lapse if price falls below exercise price All stages; most common across India
