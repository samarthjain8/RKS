Understanding M&A

What Are Mergers
& Acquisitions?

Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A) refer to transactions where two companies combine their operations, assets, and ownership — either through an acquisition (one company buying another) or a merger (two companies joining to form a single entity).

M&A is one of the most powerful strategic tools available to a business. When executed well, it accelerates growth, eliminates competition, unlocks new markets, acquires critical technology or talent, and creates significant shareholder value far beyond what organic growth alone could achieve.

"The right acquisition at the right price can compress years of organic growth into a single transaction. The wrong one can undo decades of value creation."

Conversely, poorly structured or mispriced M&A transactions are among the leading destroyers of corporate value. The difference lies in the quality of deal origination, due diligence, valuation, negotiation, and post-merger integration — all areas where RKS Investments brings deep, hands-on expertise.

◆

Accelerated Growth

Acquiring a competitor, supplier, or complementary business compresses years of organic growth into months — adding revenue, capacity, and capability simultaneously.

◆

Market Entry & Expansion

M&A provides instant access to new geographies, customer bases, and distribution networks that would take years to build independently.

◆

Technology & IP Acquisition

Acquiring businesses for their intellectual property, proprietary technology, or R&D pipeline is often faster and cheaper than building in-house.

◆

Promoter Exit & Liquidity

For founders seeking a partial or complete exit, a strategic acquisition offers the highest valuations and fastest liquidity compared to other exit routes.

◆

Synergy & Cost Efficiency

Merging operations eliminates duplication, achieves economies of scale, strengthens procurement power, and improves combined profitability.

◆

Talent & Management Acquisition

Acqui-hires — acquiring companies primarily for their leadership team or specialist workforce — are increasingly common in competitive sectors.

Deal Classifications

Types of M&A Transactions

01
Horizontal

Horizontal M&A

A merger or acquisition between two companies operating in the same industry and often at the same stage of production. The primary driver is market consolidation, competitive elimination, or achieving scale.

02
Vertical

Vertical M&A

Combining companies at different stages of the same supply chain — a manufacturer acquiring a supplier (backward integration) or a distributor (forward integration) — to control costs and margin.

03
Conglomerate

Conglomerate M&A

A transaction between companies in entirely unrelated businesses — typically driven by portfolio diversification, capital deployment, or group synergies. Common in Indian business groups.

04
Cross-Border

Cross-Border M&A

Transactions involving an Indian company acquiring a foreign business, or a foreign company acquiring an Indian target — subject to FEMA, SEBI, and sector-specific FDI regulations.

An Open Offer is triggered when an acquirer crosses the 25% shareholding threshold (or makes a creeping acquisition beyond permitted limits) in a listed company under SEBI's Takeover Code (SAST Regulations, 2011).

Open offers are complex, time-critical, and highly regulated — requiring a registered merchant banker as manager to the open offer. RKS Investments coordinates and supports the full open offer process in conjunction with the BRLM.

We also advise on voluntary open offers, competing offers, and delisting triggers that arise post-acquisition — ensuring compliance and optimal outcome for the acquirer client.

Due Diligence
Framework

Due diligence is the backbone of any successful M&A transaction. It is the systematic process of verifying every material claim made about a business before committing capital — uncovering hidden risks, confirming value drivers, and providing the factual basis for negotiations.

RKS Investments coordinates a comprehensive due diligence exercise across all critical dimensions of the target business. We work with the client's legal, tax, and operational advisors — managing the data room, tracking open items, and producing a consolidated findings report that forms the basis for price adjustment, warranty carve-outs, and condition precedents.

Our approach is proportionate — calibrated to deal size, sector risk, and transaction timeline. We focus on the material issues that move valuation and deal terms, not box-ticking exercises.

Pricing a Business

Valuation Methodologies We Use

DCF

Discounted Cash Flow

Values the business on the present value of projected future free cash flows, discounted at the weighted average cost of capital (WACC). Best for businesses with predictable, long-term cash flows.

Ideal for: Stable businesses, infrastructure, annuity models
CCA

Comparable Company Analysis

Benchmarks the target against publicly listed peers using trading multiples such as EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue, and P/E — grounding valuation in live market data.

Ideal for: Most transaction types as a sanity check
CTA

Comparable Transaction Analysis

References valuation multiples paid in recent M&A deals involving similar companies — capturing the control premium typically paid in acquisitions over public market trading values.

Ideal for: Acquisition pricing, fairness opinions
NAV

Net Asset Value

Values the business based on the fair value of its underlying assets minus liabilities. Particularly relevant for asset-heavy businesses, holding companies, and real estate firms.

Ideal for: Asset-heavy companies, holding entities
