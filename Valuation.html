Understanding Valuations

What Is a Business
Valuation?

A business valuation is the process of determining the economic worth of a company or a business interest — expressed as a range of value, a specific number, or a price per share. It draws on financial analysis, market data, regulatory requirements, and professional judgment to arrive at a figure that is supportable, defensible, and fit for purpose.

Valuations are not a single number — they are a function of purpose, methodology, and timing. The fair value of a business for an M&A transaction may differ from its regulatory value under FEMA, its tax value under the Income Tax Act, or its financial reporting value under Ind AS. Each context demands a different analytical lens.

"A valuation is only as good as the assumptions behind it. Transparency, rigor, and independence are what make a report defensible when it truly matters."

RKS Investments delivers valuations that are not merely arithmetically correct but analytically rigorous and narratively compelling — reports that withstand scrutiny from regulators, tax authorities, investors, and courts.

Analytical Frameworks

Valuation Methodologies
We Apply

No single methodology tells the complete story of a business's value. RKS Investments applies the most appropriate primary method for each engagement — cross-checked against secondary approaches — to arrive at a triangulated, defensible conclusion of value.

DCF
Income Approach

Discounted Cash Flow

Values the business by discounting projected free cash flows to the firm (FCFF) back to their present value using the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC). The terminal value — representing cash flows beyond the explicit forecast period — is computed using either the Gordon Growth Model or an exit multiple.

1
Forecast revenue, EBITDA, capex, and working capital for 5–10 years
2
Compute FCFF and discount at WACC (cost of equity + cost of debt)
3
Add terminal value; subtract net debt to arrive at equity value
4
Sensitivity analysis across WACC and terminal growth rate assumptions
Best Suited For Businesses with predictable, long-term cash flows — manufacturing, infrastructure, SaaS, financials, and regulated sectors.
CCA
Market Approach

Comparable Company Analysis

Benchmarks the subject company against a set of publicly listed peers using trading multiples — EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue, P/E, and P/BV — adjusted for size, growth, and profitability differentials. Provides a market-grounded reality check on intrinsic value.

1
Identify the most relevant publicly listed peer set
2
Compute LTM and forward multiples for each peer
3
Apply median / selected multiple to subject company metrics
4
Apply size, liquidity, and control premiums / discounts
Best Suited For Most transaction types — used as a primary or cross-check method. Essential for regulatory valuations under SEBI and FEMA.
CTA
Market Approach

Comparable Transaction Analysis

References acquisition multiples paid in recent M&A transactions involving comparable businesses — capturing the control premium typically embedded in deal prices. Useful for M&A advisory where deal pricing precedent is the most relevant benchmark.

1
Identify recent comparable M&A transactions in same or adjacent sectors
2
Extract deal multiples — EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue, P/BV at announcement
3
Adjust for deal structure, strategic rationale, and market conditions
4
Apply to subject company to derive implied enterprise / equity value
Best Suited For M&A transactions, fairness opinions, and PE exit pricing — particularly in sectors with active deal activity.
NAV
Asset Approach

Net Asset Value

Values the business on the fair value of its underlying assets minus its total liabilities. Adjusts historical book values to their current fair market values — particularly for land, property, investments, and intangibles that may be significantly under- or over-stated on the balance sheet.

1
Restate each balance sheet asset to fair market value
2
Identify and value off-balance-sheet assets and contingent liabilities
3
Deduct fair value of liabilities from adjusted asset value
4
Apply control premium or minority discount as appropriate
Best Suited For Asset-heavy businesses — real estate, manufacturing, holding companies — and situations where going-concern assumptions are unreliable.
EBO
Income Approach

Excess Earnings / EBO Method

Separates the return attributable to tangible assets from the return attributable to intangible assets — particularly useful when valuing businesses where goodwill, brand, or relationships constitute a significant portion of total value. Commonly applied in partnership disputes and professional practice valuations.

1
Determine normalised earnings of the business
2
Calculate fair return on tangible assets at appropriate rate
3
Remaining "excess" earnings = return from intangibles (goodwill)
4
Capitalise excess earnings at risk-adjusted intangible asset rate
Best Suited For Professional services firms, franchise businesses, goodwill impairment testing, and partnership dispute valuations.
OPM
Options Approach

Option Pricing Models

Applies financial option theory — Black-Scholes or the Binomial model — to value equity in complex capital structures, early-stage companies, convertible securities, and situations where equity behaves like a call option on the firm's assets. Also used for ESOP fair value determination under Ind AS 102.

1
Model firm value, volatility, and time to liquidity event
2
Allocate enterprise value across share classes using waterfall
3
Apply DLOM (discount for lack of marketability) if appropriate
4
Derive per-share fair value for each class of security
Best Suited For ESOP valuations, complex PE-backed capital structures (CCPS, warrants), and early-stage startups with uncertain outcomes.

Compliance & Regulation

Regulatory Valuation
Requirements

Indian companies face an extensive — and often overlapping — web of regulatory valuation requirements. A valuation prepared for one purpose (say, a PE fundraise) may not satisfy the requirements of another (a FEMA filing), even if the underlying business is the same.

Each regulatory framework prescribes specific methodology rules, eligibility of the valuer, required disclosures, and the form of the opinion. Non-compliance can result in transactions being voided, penalties levied, or tax liabilities crystallised.

RKS Investments ensures that every valuation report is structured, certified, and disclosed in precise compliance with the applicable regulatory framework — giving our clients the certainty they need.

FEMA 20R
FDI pricing — internationally accepted pricing methodology Required for issue / transfer of shares to / from non-residents. SEBI-registered MB or CA mandatory as valuer.
IT Act Rule 11UA
Preferential allotment price — DCF or NAV (Option B / A) Applicable to issue of shares to residents / non-residents; Section 56(2)(viib) anti-abuse provisions.
SEBI ICDR
Preferential issue pricing — floor price under SEBI formula Based on 26/52-week VWAP for listed companies; merchant banker valuation for unlisted entities.
SEBI SAST
Open offer price — highest of negotiated, 26W VWAP, or book value Manager to the open offer must certify compliance; independent valuation for book value floor.
NCLT / Companies Act
Merger swap ratio — registered valuer mandatory Registered Valuer (IBBI) required for scheme of arrangement; fairness opinion from independent advisor expected.
Ind AS 103
Purchase price allocation — fair value of assets and liabilities Required on acquisition of a business; triggers intangible asset identification and goodwill computation.
Ind AS 102
ESOP fair value — Black-Scholes or Binomial model Annual ESOP expense requires independent fair value of options at grant date; volatility and risk-free rate assumptions critical.
Insolvency (IBC)
Liquidation value and fair value — IBBI Registered Valuer Two registered valuers required for IBC proceedings; valuation forms the basis of resolution plan evaluation.
What Moves Value

Key Value Drivers
We Analyse

A valuation is not merely a mathematical exercise. It is an analytical investigation into what makes a specific business worth more or less than its peers — and why value may differ from what the financial statements alone suggest.

Understanding value drivers allows management to take targeted action to increase enterprise value before a transaction or fundraise — making the timing, preparation, and framing of a valuation engagement strategically important, not just technically necessary.

RKS Investments's valuation team explicitly identifies and quantifies each material value driver and discount, giving clients a clear understanding of what is driving their current valuation — and what can be done to improve it.

Revenue
Scale & Growth Rate

Revenue trajectory, CAGR, and market share trajectory are the primary determinants of enterprise value multiples in most sectors.

Profitability
EBITDA Margin & Quality

Margin level, stability, and trend — adjusted for one-offs — determine the earnings base against which multiples are applied.

Capital Efficiency
ROCE & Asset Turns

Businesses with high return on capital and low reinvestment needs command premium valuations in DCF and exit multiple frameworks.

Competitive Moat
Defensibility & IP

Sustainable competitive advantages — IP, switching costs, network effects, regulatory licences — reduce earnings volatility and justify higher multiples.

Customer Base
Concentration & Retention

High customer concentration is a discount factor; high retention, recurring revenue, and diversification are premium drivers.

Management
Team Depth & Succession

Promoter-dependent businesses attract key-person risk discounts; institutionalised, depth-management teams are valued at a premium.

Balance Sheet
Net Debt & Working Capital

Excess cash and low net debt add directly to equity value; over-leveraged or working-capital-heavy businesses face valuation discounts.

Governance
Transparency & Compliance

Clean audits, no contingent liabilities, strong board governance, and compliant books attract premium valuations from institutional buyers.

Our Valuation Process

Every valuation engagement follows a rigorous, structured process designed to produce a defensible, high-quality report — on time and calibrated precisely to its intended purpose.

01

Engagement Scoping & Purpose Confirmation

We begin by understanding the specific purpose of the valuation — regulatory, transactional, financial reporting, or strategic — and confirming the appropriate standard of value (fair value, fair market value, investment value), the effective date, and the interest being valued (minority, majority, or 100%).

Purpose clarification Standard of value Effective date Engagement letter
Duration: 1 – 2 Days
02

Information Gathering & Management Discussion

We collect audited financial statements (3–5 years), management accounts, business plans, customer and supplier data, legal documents, cap table, and any prior valuation reports. A detailed management discussion is held to understand industry dynamics, competitive positioning, and future plans — information not visible in financial statements alone.

Audited financials Business plan Management interview Market data
Duration: 3 – 5 Days
03

Financial Analysis & Normalisation

We analyse and normalise the historical financials — adjusting for one-off items, related-party transactions at non-arm's-length, owner perks, and accounting policy differences — to arrive at a clean, representative earnings base. This normalised base forms the foundation for all forward projections and multiple applications.

One-off adjustments Related-party normalisation Working capital analysis EBITDA bridge
Duration: 2 – 4 Days
04

Methodology Selection & Model Building

We select the most appropriate primary and secondary methodologies based on the business type, purpose, and data availability. Financial models are built in full — including the 5-year DCF model with WACC derivation, peer multiple analysis with LTM and forward metrics, and NAV computation where relevant.

DCF model WACC derivation Peer multiple table NAV computation
Duration: 3 – 6 Days
05

Triangulation & Concluded Value

Results from each methodology are synthesised — applying appropriate weightings based on the reliability of inputs and the purpose of the valuation — to arrive at a triangulated concluded value or value range. A sensitivity matrix is constructed to show how the conclusion responds to key assumption changes.

Method weighting Value range determination Sensitivity matrix Concluded value
Duration: 1 – 2 Days

Report Drafting, Review & Delivery

A full valuation report is drafted — covering the scope of engagement, company background, industry overview, financial analysis, methodology description, analytical outputs, limitations, and the concluded opinion of value. The report is reviewed internally and discussed with the client before finalisation and formal issuance.

Beyond Tangible Assets

Intangible Asset
Valuation

For most modern businesses, the majority of enterprise value resides in intangible assets — brands, customer relationships, proprietary technology, and intellectual property — that do not appear on the balance sheet at fair value under historical cost accounting standards.

When a business is acquired, Ind AS 103 requires the acquirer to identify and value these intangibles separately from goodwill in a Purchase Price Allocation exercise. This process has direct implications for post-acquisition depreciation charges and goodwill impairment risk.

RKS Investments has deep experience identifying, classifying, and valuing intangible assets using the most appropriate methodology for each type — delivering reports that satisfy auditor and regulatory scrutiny.

◆
Brand & Trademark

Valued using the Relief from Royalty method — estimating the royalty the business would pay to license the brand if it did not own it, and capitalising those royalty savings.

◆
Customer Relationships & Lists

Valued using the Multi-Period Excess Earnings Method (MEEM) — projecting revenues attributable to the existing customer base and discounting at a risk-adjusted rate reflecting customer attrition.

◆
Technology & IP

Valued using Relief from Royalty (for licensable technology) or the Cost to Recreate method — estimating the cost, time, and risk of developing equivalent technology from scratch.

◆
Non-Compete & Non-Solicit Agreements

Valued using the With-and-Without method — comparing the enterprise value with the non-compete in place versus the value that would be lost if the seller were free to compete.

◆
Favourable Contracts & Licences

Valued based on the present value of above-market cash flows attributable to favourable supply, distribution, or regulatory licence agreements relative to current market rates.
